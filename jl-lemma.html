Dimensionality Reduction

Johnson–Lindenstrauss Lemma

You can squash high-dimensional data into far fewer dimensions while almost perfectly preserving all pairwise distances — using a single random matrix.

Section 1

The Core Idea

Imagine you have $n$ points scattered in a very high-dimensional space — say, documents as 100,000-dimensional word-count vectors, or images as millions of pixels. Many algorithms scale badly with dimension. Can we shrink the space?

Naively, projecting onto fewer dimensions destroys structure. But in 1984, Johnson and Lindenstrauss proved something remarkable:

Johnson–Lindenstrauss Lemma (informal)

For any $\varepsilon \in (0,1)$ and any $n$ points in $\mathbb{R}^d$, there exists a linear map $f: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^k$ with $k = O\!\left(\frac{\log n}{\varepsilon^2}\right)$ such that for all pairs of points $u, v$:

$$(1-\varepsilon)\|u - v\|^2 \;\leq\; \|f(u) - f(v)\|^2 \;\leq\; (1+\varepsilon)\|u - v\|^2$$

The target dimension $k$ depends only on $\log n$ and $\varepsilon$ — not on the original dimension $d$!

📐

Distance Preserving

All $\binom{n}{2}$ pairwise distances are preserved within a factor of $(1 \pm \varepsilon)$.

🎲

Randomized

The projection is a random Gaussian matrix — no need to look at the data to construct it!

📦

Dimension-Independent

The target dimension $k \approx \frac{8 \ln n}{\varepsilon^2}$ doesn't depend on the original $d$.

⚡

Efficient

Constructing and applying the projection takes $O(dk)$ time — fast in practice.

Section 3

Why Random Projections Work

The magic comes from two key ideas: concentration of measure and the union bound. Let's build up the intuition.

3.1 — A single dot product concentrates

Fix a unit vector $u \in \mathbb{R}^d$. Project it onto a random unit vector $r$: the result $\langle r, u \rangle$ is a random variable.

When we use $k$ random projections, we get:

$$\|f(u)\|^2 = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=1}^{k} \langle r_i, u \rangle^2$$

Each $\langle r_i, u \rangle \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ (if $r_i$ has Gaussian entries), so each term is $\chi^2(1)$ distributed with mean 1. By the law of large numbers, the average concentrates around 1. The concentration is exponentially fast — this is the key!

3.2 — Union bound over all pairs

We need the projection to preserve all $\binom{n}{2}$ pairwise distances simultaneously. By the union bound, the probability that any pair fails is at most:

$$P[\text{some pair fails}] \leq \binom{n}{2} \cdot P[\text{one pair fails}] \leq n^2 \cdot e^{-c \varepsilon^2 k}$$

Setting $k = \frac{C \log n}{\varepsilon^2}$ makes this probability less than 1 (or any constant $\delta$). So a random projection of this dimension works with positive probability — hence it exists!

1

Gaussian entries concentrate

Each $\langle r_i, u - v\rangle^2$ is a $\chi^2$ variable. The sum of $k$ such terms concentrates around $k \|u-v\|^2$ with exponential tails.

2

Sub-gaussian tail bound

$P\!\left[\left|\frac{\|f(u-v)\|^2}{\|u-v\|^2} - 1\right| > \varepsilon\right] \leq 2e^{-(\varepsilon^2 - \varepsilon^3)k/4}$

3

Union over $\binom{n}{2}$ pairs

Multiply failure probability by $n^2$. Choose $k = \frac{8 \ln n}{\varepsilon^2}$ to make total failure probability $< \frac{1}{2}$.

4

Probabilistic argument

Since failure probability $< 1$, a good projection must exist. In practice, a random one works with high probability.

Sketch of the tail bound proof

Let $X = \sum_{i=1}^k Z_i^2$ where $Z_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ i.i.d. We want to bound $P[|X/k - 1| > \varepsilon]$. Using the moment generating function of $\chi^2$:

$$M_{Z^2}(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tZ^2}] = (1-2t)^{-1/2} \quad \text{for } t < \tfrac{1}{2}$$

So $M_X(t) = (1-2t)^{-k/2}$. Using Markov's inequality on $e^{tX}$:

$$P[X \geq (1+\varepsilon)k] \leq \frac{e^{-t(1+\varepsilon)k}}{(1-2t)^{-k/2}}$$

Optimizing over $t \in (0, \tfrac{1}{2})$ gives $t = \frac{\varepsilon}{2(1+\varepsilon)}$, and after simplification:

$$P[X \geq (1+\varepsilon)k] \leq \left(\frac{e^\varepsilon}{(1+\varepsilon)^{1+\varepsilon}}\right)^{k/2} \leq e^{-(\varepsilon^2-\varepsilon^3)k/4}$$

A symmetric bound holds for $P[X \leq (1-\varepsilon)k]$, completing the proof.

Section 4

The Formal Statement

Theorem (Johnson–Lindenstrauss, 1984)

Let $\varepsilon \in (0,1)$, $\delta \in (0,1)$, and let $X = \{x_1, \ldots, x_n\} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$. Choose $k \geq \frac{8 \ln(n/\delta)}{\varepsilon^2}$. Let $R \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times d}$ have i.i.d. $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ entries, and define $f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{k}} R x$.

Then with probability at least $1 - \delta$, for all pairs $i \neq j$:

$$(1-\varepsilon)\|x_i - x_j\|^2 \leq \|f(x_i) - f(x_j)\|^2 \leq (1+\varepsilon)\|x_i - x_j\|^2$$

Required Dimension

The minimum $k$ needed grows as:

$$k = O\!\left(\frac{\log n}{\varepsilon^2}\right)$$

This is independent of $d$! A billion-dimensional space with 1000 points can be reduced to roughly $k \approx 700$ dimensions (for $\varepsilon = 0.1$) with no loss of geometric structure.

Required target dimension $k$ vs. parameters

Notice: even for $n = 10^6$ points with $\varepsilon = 0.1$ and $\delta = 0.01$, the required dimension is only $k \approx \frac{8 \cdot 14}{0.01} \approx 11{,}200$. This is tiny compared to typical $d = 10^6$ image/text embeddings!

Section 6

Is the Bound Tight?

Yes! Alon (2003) showed that the dependence on $\log n$ is optimal: there exist $n$-point sets where any embedding with distortion $(1+\varepsilon)$ requires $k = \Omega\!\left(\frac{\log n}{\varepsilon^2 \log(1/\varepsilon)}\right)$ dimensions.

The $1/\varepsilon^2$ factor is also essentially tight — you cannot get away with fewer dimensions and still preserve all distances within $\varepsilon$.

Variants and Improvements

Fast JL (Ailon & Chazelle)

Replace dense Gaussian matrix with $\text{HD}$ (Hadamard + random signs). Runtime: $O(d \log d)$ vs $O(dk)$.

Sparse JL

Use sparse random matrices (Achlioptas 2003, Kane-Nelson 2014) — most entries zero, faster to apply.

Oblivious Subspace Embeddings

Generalization: preserve all vectors in a subspace, not just a finite set.

Randomized SVD

Use JL as subroutine for fast approximate SVD / PCA on large matrices.

Section 7

Applications

🔍

Approximate Nearest Neighbors

Project to $k \approx O(\log n)$ dims, run exact NN search there. Used in FAISS, Annoy, HNSW.

🧮

Compressed Sensing

Random projections form nearly-isometric embeddings needed for exact sparse recovery (RIP property).

📊

Clustering & Classification

Speed up $k$-means, SVMs, linear regression by reducing dimension while preserving structure.

🔐

Privacy / Sketching

Random projections can obfuscate original data while preserving statistics — used in differential privacy.

🤖

Neural Networks

Random projection layers, random feature approximations (Rahimi–Recht), kernel methods.

📝

NLP / Text Search

Random indexing, LSH for document similarity, faster transformers with random attention.
