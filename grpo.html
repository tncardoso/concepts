Group Relative Policy Optimization

GRPO — a critic-free reinforcement learning algorithm for training language models

1 Motivation: RL for LLMs

Language models trained with supervised learning learn to imitate data, but can't easily optimize for goals that are hard to specify as labels — like mathematical correctness, reasoning quality, or code that passes tests. Reinforcement learning lets us train against a reward signal instead.

The dominant algorithm for this is PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization), but PPO requires training a separate critic network (also called a value function) that estimates how good each state is. For LLMs this critic must be another large model — expensive and memory-hungry.

GRPO, introduced by DeepSeek in 2024, eliminates the critic entirely by computing a baseline from the rewards of a group of responses sampled for the same prompt.

PPO

  • Requires a value/critic network
  • Critic is ~same size as policy
  • Advantage = reward − V(state)
  • Learns V via TD/GAE updates
  • High memory overhead
  • Complex, many hyperparameters

GRPO

  • No critic network needed
  • Baseline from reward statistics
  • Advantage = normalized group reward
  • Simple mean/std calculation
  • ~Half the memory of PPO
  • Fewer moving parts

2 The Key Idea: Group Sampling

For every training prompt q, GRPO samples a group of G independent outputs from the current policy. Each output is scored by a reward model (or a rule-based function). The group's mean and standard deviation become the baseline — no separate network required.

What is 17 × 24?

Each response is treated as an independent sample from the same prompt. The mean reward of the group serves as a "how-would-an-average-response-do" baseline — intuitively, responses that beat the group average get positive advantage, and those that fall below get negative advantage.

3 Advantage Estimation

The advantage Âi for the i-th response in a group of G is:

Âi = ri − mean({r1,…,rG}) std({r1,…,rG})

This is simply z-score normalization over the group rewards. It answers: "How much better or worse was this response than the group average, in units of standard deviation?"

Why normalize? Normalizing by the group std keeps gradients well-scaled regardless of the reward function's magnitude. A reward model that outputs values in [0, 10] and one that outputs [0, 1000] will produce the same normalized advantages.

4 The GRPO Objective

GRPO maximizes the following objective (written as a loss to minimize):

ℒGRPO = − 1 G ∑i=1G min( ρi · Âi,  clip(ρi, 1−ε, 1+ε) · Âi ) + β · 𝔻KL
ρi = πθ(oi|q) / πθold(oi|q)
Probability ratio: how much more/less likely is the new policy to produce this response compared to the old policy?
Âi
Group-relative advantage: normalized reward for response i within its group.
clip(ρ, 1−ε, 1+ε)
Prevents the policy from changing too rapidly. ε is typically 0.1–0.2.
β · 𝔻KL(πθ ‖ πref)
Penalizes divergence from a reference (initial) policy to prevent reward hacking.

The min between the clipped and unclipped objectives creates a pessimistic bound: it only uses the unclipped ratio when doing so would reduce the objective (i.e., discourage bad behavior), but blocks the policy from taking large steps toward good behavior.

5 Clipping the Probability Ratio

The clipping mechanism is the heart of the PPO-style update. It creates a trust region without solving a constrained optimization problem.

Interactive: Clipped Objective

─ Unclipped   ─ Clipped   ─ min(unclipped, clipped) = actual objective

Intuition: When the advantage is positive (response is good), clipping caps how much we can increase its probability — preventing overconfident updates. When advantage is negative (response is bad), clipping prevents the policy from being pushed too far away from its current behavior all at once.

6 KL Divergence Penalty

Without any constraint, a policy trained purely on reward can collapse into degenerate behavior — outputting whatever maximally satisfies the reward model, even if it's nonsense. The KL divergence penalty keeps the policy close to a frozen reference policy (usually the supervised fine-tuned model before RL training).

𝔻KL(πθ ‖ πref) = ∑t πθ(at | st) · log πθ(at | st) πref(at | st)

The β coefficient controls the strength of this penalty. A larger β keeps the policy closer to the reference but slows improvement. In practice, DeepSeek uses a small β (around 0.001–0.1) so the KL term acts as a soft regularizer rather than a hard constraint.

7 The Training Loop

Each iteration of GRPO processes a batch of prompts and performs the following steps:

📥
Sample prompt batch
Draw a batch of prompts from the training dataset.
batch = dataset.sample(batch_size=16)
🔀
Generate G responses per prompt
Sample G independent completions from the current policy and cache their log-probabilities — these become the fixed denominator for ratio computation during updates.
responses, old_log_probs = policy.generate_with_logprobs(prompts, n=G)
🏆
Score with reward model
Compute a scalar reward for each response using a reward function or model.
rewards = reward_fn(prompts, responses) # shape [B, G]
📊
Compute group-relative advantages
Normalize rewards within each group to get advantages.
A = (rewards − rewards.mean(-1, keepdim=True)) / rewards.std(-1)
⚙️
Compute GRPO loss
Calculate the clipped surrogate loss plus KL penalty under the current policy πθ.
ratio = (policy.log_prob(responses) - old_log_probs).exp()
loss = -mean(min(ratio*A, clip(ratio,1-ε,1+ε)*A)) + β*KL
🔧
Gradient update
Backpropagate and update only the policy network. No critic to update.
optimizer.step() # ← only one model to update!

✓ Summary

GRPO achieves PPO-quality RL training for language models by replacing the critic with a simple statistical trick: normalizing rewards within a group of responses sampled for the same prompt. This eliminates an entire large model from the training pipeline.

The algorithm inherits PPO's stability through clipped probability ratios, and prevents reward hacking through a KL divergence penalty against a frozen reference policy.

GRPO was used to train DeepSeek-R1 and its distilled variants, and has since become a popular foundation for open RLHF training pipelines like TRL, OpenRLHF, and veRL.
